Hey Aubrey,

Symmetric divisions — fair. Here's what I think the data actually show.

Katajisto et al. (2015, PMID 25837514) watched human mammary stemlike cells sort their mitochondria at division: young ones to the stem cell daughter, old ones to the differentiating daughter. Stem cells segregate aged organelles asymmetrically. The centriole follows the same logic (Yamashita 2007), with one difference — it templates its own structure.

Symmetric divisions add young-centriole stem cells to the pool but don't remove old centrioles from their lineage. Bener et al. (2026, PMID 42056503) showed that in the Drosophila germline niche, asymmetric divisions dominate the dynamics — they slow clonal expansion and preserve heterogeneity. The stem cell carrying the old mother centriole persists. Its centriole keeps aging.

And the templating problem doesn't care about dilution rate. A damaged centriole propagates structural errors to its daughter from new proteins. Reducing the frequency of old centrioles in the pool helps — but it doesn't fix the template in the cells that still carry one.

If dilution through symmetric divisions solved the problem, the oocyte wouldn't need to eliminate centrioles at all. But it does — completely. That's the fact I keep coming back to.

I'll rework the handout to lead with stem cell centrioles and flag the symmetric division caveat.
