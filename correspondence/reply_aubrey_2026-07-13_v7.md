Hey Aubrey,

One last thought on the perpendicular point.

The SAS-6 ring assembles flat against the mother's surface at the proximal end — the ring plane is parallel to the surface. The procentriole then grows outward from that plane, which is why mother and daughter end up orthogonal. The surface templates the ring; the ring sets the daughter's cross-section.

Gomez Melo et al. (2023) modelled this. SAS-6 ring assembly on a surface is faster than in solution by four orders of magnitude. The surface isn't just a platform.

So the defect type matters. Surface changes at the proximal end — where polyE, carbonylation, and ALMS1 seeds sit — should propagate. Over-elongation of the mother is a different thing and probably doesn't. Good catch on the distinction.
