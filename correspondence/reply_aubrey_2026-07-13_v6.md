Hey Aubrey,

The two-step objection is the right one. Let me walk through why I think it's one mechanism, not two.

The cartwheel assembles against the mother's surface. If the surface is altered, the cartwheel starts from altered geometry. That daughter becomes a mother — same mechanism, same surface, next cycle. One bent mold, N bent castings. No extra improbability.

The real question is whether polyE, carbonylation, and ALMS1 condensates actually change the surface enough to affect the cartwheel. If they don't, no propagation. If they block assembly, no daughter. Only if they alter geometry without blocking does propagation happen. I think the data now favour the third option.

Dittrich et al. (2023) used high-throughput electron tomography to look at 1,386 centrioles from healthy donors across age. They found structural aberrations accumulating with age — mother centrioles over-elongating to 1,654 nm. Direct measurement, not inference. Centrioles change structurally as they get older.

The molecular pathway is coming into focus. Ozaki & Tsou (2026) showed that ALMS1 — an intrinsically disordered protein that forms condensates on the mother centriole — controls cartwheel assembly. Remove it, cartwheels don't form. Put it back, they do — but the resulting centrioles have "diverse yet heritable architectures that evolve through selection." The architecture depends on the seed. The same group identified the seeds: CEP152-CEP63-PCNT aggregates, assembled by ALMS1 on the mother surface. A changed surface assembles a changed seed, which templates a changed cartwheel.

Banterle et al. (2021) watched SAS-6 rings form on surfaces in real time with high-speed AFM. The surface properties determine the ring geometry.

So the chain runs: age-damaged surface → altered seeds → altered cartwheel → altered daughter. One mechanism throughout.

I'm not claiming this is proven. The experiment — cryo-ET of the proximal surface of centrioles from young vs aged fibroblasts, measuring cartwheel geometry directly — hasn't been done. But the methods exist. Fung et al. (2025) already did whole-centrosome cryo-ET at molecular resolution.

If you still see a hidden assumption I'm missing, I want to know.
