Hey Aubrey,

Your premise about dividing cells has an exception — and it's the one that matters. Stem cells divide asymmetrically. The stem cell daughter keeps the old mother centriole. The differentiating daughter gets the new one (Yamashita 2007, Royall 2023). So synthesis doesn't refresh the centriole in the cell that has to last a lifetime. The old template is actively retained in the stem cell compartment.

On NPCs — a damaged NPC is a damaged NPC. It stops there. A damaged centriole templates its geometry onto the daughter. Even from new proteins, the daughter inherits the mother's structural errors. That's not turnover with dismantling. That's propagation.

Nobody has measured the half-life of centriolar tubulin in a human neuron. But the oocyte eliminates and rebuilds. It doesn't wait for turnover. I take that as a hint.

Does this hold?
