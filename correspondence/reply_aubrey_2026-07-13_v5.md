Hey Aubrey,

Flies aren't mammals — conceded. And teleology was sloppy — also conceded. But the templating claim is the one I want to defend.

Centriole duplication is templated, not de novo. The daughter assembles on the mother's surface. PLK4 docks at a ring of CEP152 and CEP192 at the mother's proximal end; STIL and SAS-6 then build the cartwheel — the 9-fold scaffold — against that surface (Nigg & Holland 2018). The mother is never taken apart. The daughter starts from whatever geometry the mother presents.

Over time, the mother's surface picks up polyglutamylation, carbonylation, and ALMS1 condensates (Ozaki & Tsou 2026). Older centrioles carry measurably more polyE than young ones (Sullenberger et al. 2020). A surface that changes chemically changes geometrically — and it's the template for the next cartwheel.

This isn't confined to stem cells. Senescence causes centrosome amplification and cilium defects in fully differentiated cells (Muhammad et al. 2021). Centrioles age wherever they sit.

Separately — and this doesn't depend on mechanism — most plants lack somatic centrioles. And a carrot cell makes a whole carrot. Animal cells keep their centrioles and can't do that. It's a correlation, not a proof. But it's a striking one.

The direct experiment — damage a centriole, watch it duplicate in fresh cytoplasm, measure the daughter by cryo-ET — hasn't been done. So the claim is an inference. But the mechanism it rests on is solid. If the template is bent, the part comes out bent.

If I'm wrong about how duplication works, tell me. That's the linchpin.
