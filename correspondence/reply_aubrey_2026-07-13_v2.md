Hey Aubrey,

NPCs — fair. But there's an asymmetry here that I think matters.

When a stem cell divides asymmetrically, the stem cell daughter keeps the old mother centriole. The differentiating daughter gets the new one (Yamashita 2007, Royall 2023). The centriole in the stem cell compartment — the only dividing cells that last a lifetime — is never refreshed by synthesis. It's retained on purpose. Over decades of divisions, it accumulates damage the daughter's fresh centriole never sees.

NPCs age evenly across the tissue. Centrioles in stem cells don't: the oldest template is actively concentrated in the self-renewing compartment.

If that's right, the oocyte's strategy — eliminate and rebuild, rather than wait for turnover — isn't a curiosity. It's the only route out.

Does this hold?
