# The Entropy Minimum

## **Entropy in Aging · Cologne · 2026**

The first blastomeres, before they assemble anything new, have the lowest entropy of any cell. A somatic cell sits at the other extreme - decades of wear in every organelle, every protein complex, every polymer. Nature clears the slate. The centriole is one structure it dismantles and rebuilds.

**The centriole problem is in stem cells.** Cells actively degrade old proteins, damaged mitochondria, and worn organelles. Autophagy, the proteasome, mitochondrial fission — each has a disposal pathway. The centriole has none. During asymmetric division, the stem cell daughter keeps the old mother centriole — the new one goes to the differentiating daughter. This was first shown in Drosophila (Yamashita et al., 2007) and later confirmed in human neural progenitors (Royall et al., 2023). Over a lifetime of asymmetric divisions, the stem cell selectively accumulates the oldest centrioles. Synthesis does not refresh the centriole in the cell that has to last a lifetime. Symmetric divisions add young centrioles to the pool but do not remove old ones from their lineage. This is not confined to stem cells: high-throughput electron tomography of 1,386 centrioles revealed structural over-elongation in plasma cells, with severity correlating with disease progression (Dittrich et al., 2023). Whether similar aberrations accumulate in healthy aging somatic cells is an open question — the methods to answer it exist.

**The centriole is a geometric template.** The daughter centriole assembles on the mother's surface. ALMS1 condensates seed CEP152-CEP63-PCNT aggregates, which template cartwheel formation (Ozaki & Tsou, 2026). SAS-6 ring assembly is surface-guided - the mother's surface properties determine the daughter's cartwheel geometry (Banterle et al., 2021). A damaged surface assembles a damaged seed, which templates a damaged cartwheel. One process, start to finish.

**Plants lack somatic centrioles.** A single carrot cell makes a whole carrot. Animal cells keep their centrioles and cannot do this.

We can reset three counters of replicative aging. Telomeres. Mitochondria. The epigenome. The fourth - the centriole - has never been reset in a somatic cell.

**The experiment: Step 1**: Eliminate the old centriole. **Step 2:** Reprogram with DUX4 and TPRX1 — Totipotency. **Step 3:** Rebuild de novo centrioles.

Nature does this in every oocyte. Nobody has tried it in a somatic cell.

## Q&A

**Q:** Dividing cells synthesise new centriolar proteins - where's the accumulation? Nuclear pore complexes are also long-lived. Why is the centriole different?

**A:** The dividing cells that matter — stem cells — are the exception. They keep the old mother centriole and hand the new one to the differentiating daughter (Yamashita et al., 2007; Royall et al., 2023). Unlike an NPC, a damaged centriole passes its errors on.

**Q:** Symmetric divisions dilute old centrioles across the pool.

**A:** They add young centrioles but don't remove old ones. The centriole carrying the old template sticks around. More to the point, it's a template: ALMS1 condensates on the mother surface seed the cartwheel (Ozaki & Tsou, 2026). SAS-6 assembly is surface-guided (Banterle et al., 2021). The surface changes with age (Dittrich et al., 2023). The daughter inherits the change.

**Q:** The daughter is perpendicular to the mother. How does the same defect transfer?

**A:** The SAS-6 ring assembles flat against the mother's surface - ring plane parallel to surface. The procentriole grows outward from that plane, which is why the two end up orthogonal. A deformed surface produces a deformed ring, which produces a daughter with altered cross-sectional geometry. SAS-6 ring assembly is surface-catalysed by four orders of magnitude (Gomez Melo et al., 2023). Over-elongation of the mother is a different kind of defect and probably doesn't propagate. Surface defects do. Length defects may not.

**Q:** Is this proven?

**A:** No. The experiment — cryo-ET of the proximal surface of centrioles from young vs aged fibroblasts — hasn't been done. The methods exist (cryo-ET of centrosomes is established; see e.g. PMID 39721584). The rest is inference. Strong inference, but inference.
